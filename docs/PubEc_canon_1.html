Recap: Exchange & Consumption Efficiency

  • Edgeworth box: two consumer \((A,B)\)/two goods \((x,y)\) exchange model with endowment point \(E\)
  • Pareto efficiency: equalization of marginal rates of substitution (MRS) between both consumers: \[ MRS_A(x,y) = MRS_B(x,y) \tag{1}\]

Contract Curve

  • locus of allocations that fulfills (1)

Core

  • segment of contract curve reachable from \(E\)

Recap: First Theorem of Welfare Economics


First Theorem of Welfare Economics

Under strict conditions, a competitive market equilibrium is Pareto efficient.

  • i.o.w.: market clearing \(\implies\) efficiency

  • in the Edgeworth Box, this implies:

    • starting from endowment \(E\), market interaction and market clearing lead to equilibrium market prices that make agents pick allocation \(M\)
    • 1st Theorem implies: \(M\) is on the contract curve (in the core) and thus Pareto efficient
    • \(M\) can always be reached from \(E\)

Utility Possibilities Frontier (UPF)

  • we now ask: what are the highest level of utilities that individuals can achieve in a 2-person economy with given resources or endowments?
  • i.o.w.: how much utility can we get out of our endowment of consumption goods, at most, if we are willing to redistribute between agents?
    • increasing consumption (and, hence, utility) of \(A\), given Pareto efficiency, is associated with a decrease in consumption (and, hence, utility) of \(B\)
  • this linkage gives rise to the utility possibilities frontier (UPF)
    • UPF shows the maximum attainable level of utility for all members of society
    • analysis is: given \(u_A\), what is \(\max u_B\)? (or converse)
  • then, UPF corresponds to Pareto efficient allocations on contract curve when we consider the levels of utility

Recap: Social Welfare Function (SWF)

  • SWF: society’s ‘utility’ function
  • Bergson-Samuelson SWF is purely based on individual utilities \[ W=W(u_A,u_B)\]
  • social decisions involve trade-off across groups or individuals
    • interpersonal comparability of utilities necessary when redistributing
    • utility functions are cardinal since utility changes will matter
  • curvature of SWF reflects inequality aversion, \(\varepsilon\)
named after: Rawls Nash Bentham
example: \(W=\min\{u_A,u_B\}\) \(W=u_A\cdot u_B\) \(W=u_A+u_B\)
ineq. aversion: \(\varepsilon\to\infty\) \(0<\varepsilon<\infty\) \(\varepsilon= 0\)

First Best

  • combining SWF and UPF allows determining the First Best social choice, \(F\)
  • technically, UPF acts as a constraint on SWF

Second Theorem of Welfare Economics

  • markets in themselves may generate inequality as a side-effect
  • 1st Theorem: market equilibrium is Pareto efficient; it is silent on equity
    • how about we redistribute when market outcomes not ‘fair’ or ‘too unequal’

Second Theorem of Welfare Economics

Any Pareto efficient outcome can be decentralized as a competitive equilibrium, for some distribution of initial endowments.

  • i.o.w.: market clearing \(\impliedby\) efficiency
    • distribution function as additional role of government

Separability

  • when first best is achievable, equity and efficiency are, in fact, separable
  • needs initial, non-distortionary lump-sum taxes and transfers before market interaction starts

Social Optimality: First Best versus Second Best

Lump Sum Redistribution

  • lump-sum taxes and transfers are tied to the identity of the tax payer or transfer recipient, but not to individual’s choices (e.g.: income, consumption, wealth) and can therefore not be influenced

Second Best: Motivation

Consider a redistribution program that takes from the rich and gives to the poor.

[…] the money must be carried from the rich to the poor in a leaky bucket. Some of it will simply disappear in transit, so the poor will not receive all the money that is taken from the rich. […] I want you to decide how much leakage you would accept and still support [this plan]. Suppose 10 percent leaks out […] Should society still make the switch? If 50 percent leaks out? 75 percent? […] Where would you draw the line? Your answer cannot be right or wrong. […] Of course, the leak represents an inefficiency. The inefficiencies of real-world redistribution include the adverse effects on the economic incentives of the rich and the poor, and the administrative costs of tax-collection and transfer programs.


\(\qquad\qquad\qquad\) Arthur M. Okun, 1975,
\(\qquad\qquad\qquad\) Equality and Efficiency: The Big Tradeoff.

Second Best

  • lump-sum implies no behavioral substitution response

  • in reality, howver, all taxes and transfers are distortionary

  • illustration: redistribution implies efficiency losses that shift UPF inwards

  • Second Best, \(S\): maximize social welfare subject to the constraint that efficiency losses are unavoidable

  • trade-off: equity \(\uparrow\) \(\Rightarrow\) redistribution \(\uparrow\) \(\Rightarrow\) taxes \(\uparrow\) \(\Rightarrow\) welfare costs \(\uparrow\)

Non-Separability and the Fundamental Equity/Efficiency Trade-off

  • in a second-best world, equity and efficiency are non-separable
  • i.e., distortionary redistribution means giving up efficiency to achieve higher equity

Summary

  • Pareto efficiency is a key concept for societal welfare
    • it embodies the exhaustion of gains from trade through voluntary action
    • to achieve it, one can rely on competitive markets (1st Theorem)


  • Pareto efficiency does not account for inequality concerns
  • The social welfare function does allow to reflect inequality aversion
  • The first-best social choice combines the quest for efficiency with views on equity
  • To achieve the first best, lump-sum taxes and transfers may be needed (2nd Theorem)


  • distortionary taxation allows at most second best social choice (efficiency-equity tradeoff)
